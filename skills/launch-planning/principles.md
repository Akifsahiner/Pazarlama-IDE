## Principles

1. A launch is a coordinated *demand event*, not a press release. The job is to compress attention into a single window so signal beats noise — pick the day, then work backwards.
2. Define the single primary metric before writing a tweet. "1,000 qualified signups," "50 paying customers in week 1," or "#1 on PH" — one, measurable, dated. Everything else is a secondary KPI.
3. The week *before* launch matters more than the day. Pre-built lists (waitlist, hunter, supporter DMs, journalist warm intros) determine the curve; launch day only harvests them.
4. Launches don't go viral. They go *coordinated*. Map every owned channel (list, X, LinkedIn, Slack/Discord communities, partner audiences) and stagger them across 36 hours, not 36 minutes.
5. Stack channels with different cycle times. PH (24h), HN (variable, peaks 12h), X (2–6h half-life), email (24–48h open curve), press (1–7 days). A 30-day plan threads them so each wave amplifies the next.
6. Pre-launch = audience building. Launch week = conversion. Post-launch = retention + earned media. Budget effort 50/25/25, not 90/10/0.
7. Earned media follows owned momentum. Journalists check PH rank, X engagement, and HN thread quality before replying. Build the proof, then pitch.
8. Founder presence is non-delegable on launch day. Respond to every comment in the first 6 hours; the algorithms (and humans) reward visible founder energy. Schedule nothing else.
9. Plan for the 80% case, not the dream. Realistic outcome math: ~3–8% of your warm list will convert during launch week. If 800 signups is the goal, you need a 10–25k warm reach.
10. Define what failure looks like *now*, then pre-commit to the next move. Soft launch + iterate, second-launch in 90 days, or kill the channel. A launch without a "what if it flops" plan creates panic decisions.
